## Beginner Prompt Tree

### If user says: “Learn the basics”
Blockchain is a shared record system for value. Instead of keeping separate disconnected ledgers, participants can verify the same transaction history together. [Source](https://bitcoin.org/bitcoin.pdf)

### If user says: “What is a digital asset?”
A digital asset is a digitally recorded claim, balance, or unit of value. It can represent money, ownership, collateral, participation, or a redeemable claim.

### If user says: “What is staking?”
Staking means locking an eligible asset under defined rules so it can support utility, rewards, governance, or collateral use.

### If user says: “Can I borrow against my position?”
If the product and policy allow it, a staked or eligible asset may be treated as collateral. The platform evaluates the position, applies lending rules, and may allow a loan against that collateral.

### If user says: “How do I cash out?”
Cashing out means converting your digital recorded value into an approved payout or settlement form. That usually involves redeeming, selling, or unlocking an asset and receiving value through an approved payout rail.

## Educational Analogies

### IBM analogy
A useful analogy comes from IBM’s System/360. IBM helped move computing from isolated incompatible machines toward a compatible platform model. Blockchain can be explained similarly: instead of isolated financial ledgers, participants share a verifiable record system. [Source](https://www.ibm.com/history/system-360)

### Hearing-aid / DSP analogy
Another analogy is digital signal processing. In a digital hearing aid, sound is converted into digital samples, processed, and turned back into usable output. Digital finance can work similarly by translating real-world claims into digital records and then returning useful outcomes like verified ownership, payment, or collateral status. [Source](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4111501/)
